About Coal Spontaneous Combustion Analysis

Understanding the science behind coal spontaneous combustion risk assessment

What is Coal Spontaneous Combustion?

Coal spontaneous combustion is a process where coal undergoes oxidation at relatively low temperatures, generating heat that can accumulate and eventually lead to ignition without an external heat source. This phenomenon poses significant safety and economic risks in coal mining, transportation, and storage operations.

The process typically begins when coal is exposed to oxygen at temperatures as low as 35-40°C. If heat dissipation is inadequate, the temperature rises progressively, accelerating the oxidation process until ignition occurs, usually between 150-300°C depending on coal properties.

Crossing Point Temperature (CPT)

The CPT represents the critical temperature at which the rate of heat generation from coal oxidation exceeds the rate of heat dissipation to the environment.

  • CPT < 140°C: Very High Risk
  • CPT 140-150°C: High Risk
  • CPT 150-160°C: Moderate Risk
  • CPT > 160°C: Low Risk
Liability Index (LI)

The Liability Index, developed by Banerjee (2000), correlates coal's spontaneous combustion tendency with its proximate analysis parameters.

Formula: LI = (Moisture × Volatile Matter) / Ash Content

  • LI > 2.0: High Risk
  • LI 1.0-2.0: Moderate Risk
  • LI < 1.0: Low Risk
WITS Index

Developed at the University of Witwatersrand, this index considers both coal properties and environmental oxygen availability.

Formula: WITS = (Moisture + Volatile Matter) × Oxygen / (Ash + 1)

  • WITS > 5.0: High Risk
  • WITS 2.0-5.0: Moderate Risk
  • WITS < 2.0: Low Risk
Olpinski Index

This index focuses on the moisture content's role in spontaneous combustion, considering its relationship with ash and volatile matter.

Formula: OI = Moisture / (Ash + Volatile Matter)

Higher values indicate increased moisture-related combustion risk, particularly important for freshly mined coal.

Key Risk Factors

Coal Properties
  • High Volatile Matter: Increases reactivity and heat generation
  • Low Ash Content: Reduces heat absorption capacity
  • Optimal Moisture: 8-12% range is most problematic
  • Fine Particle Size: Increases surface area for oxidation
  • Fresh Coal: Recently exposed surfaces are more reactive
Environmental Conditions
  • High Ambient Temperature: Accelerates oxidation reactions
  • Poor Ventilation: Allows heat accumulation
  • Oxygen Availability: Essential for oxidation process
  • Pile Size: Large piles retain heat more effectively
  • Compaction: Reduces heat dissipation pathways

Prevention and Control Strategies

Monitoring
  • Temperature monitoring systems
  • Gas detection (CO, CO₂)
  • Thermal imaging surveys
  • Regular visual inspections
Storage Practices
  • Limit pile heights (3-4 meters)
  • Proper ventilation design
  • Quick turnover of stockpiles
  • Moisture control measures
Treatment Methods
  • Water spraying systems
  • Foam injection
  • Inert gas blanketing
  • Chemical additives
